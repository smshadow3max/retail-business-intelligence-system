Problem statement

Why this project matters

Retail businesses generate data from billing systems, inventory records, customer programs, online orders, and promotions. A data warehouse integrates these sources so decision-makers can measure performance, detect patterns, and improve business strategy.

Objective 01

Centralize retail data

Bring data from store sales, inventory, customers, suppliers, and promotions into one structured repository to reduce data silos.

Objective 02

Support faster decisions

Enable management to analyze daily and monthly revenue, category performance, branch efficiency, and demand trends with less manual effort.

Objective 03

Improve reporting accuracy

Use dimensional modeling and ETL validation rules so dashboards and reports remain consistent across departments.

System design

Warehouse architecture

The project follows a classic retail BI pipeline: operational data sources feed an ETL layer, then load a dimensional warehouse, which powers reports and dashboards.

1. Source systemsPOS transactions, CRM records, product catalog, stock logs, e-commerce orders, supplier data.
2. ETL processClean duplicates, standardize dates, map product categories, calculate profit, validate missing values.
3. Data warehouseStore fact_sales with dimension tables such as dim_time, dim_store, dim_product, dim_customer, dim_promotion.
4. BI layerDashboards, KPI scorecards, trend analysis, drill-down reports and management summaries.

Business insights

Example analytics outputs

Once loaded, the warehouse can generate KPIs and analytical views that support both tactical and strategic decisions.

Sales analysis

Compare daily, weekly, monthly, and yearly sales. Identify seasonal spikes, low-performing periods, and revenue growth trends.

Product performance

Rank products by sales value, quantity sold, and profit margin to identify best-sellers and weak inventory items.

Customer intelligence

Segment customers by loyalty type, purchase frequency, and average bill value for targeted promotions.

Store comparison

Measure regional sales, branch profitability, and stock turnover to improve location-based planning.

Promotion analysis

Study which campaigns increased transaction volume and whether discounts improved total profit or only revenue.

Inventory monitoring

Track slow-moving and fast-moving items to maintain stock balance and reduce overstock or stockout issues.

Implementation guide

Suggested development phases

This structure is suitable for a college submission because it clearly connects theory, schema design, ETL flow, and BI outcomes.

Phase 1 — Requirement study: Identify business questions such as total sales by region, best-selling products, customer segments, and campaign effectiveness.
Phase 2 — Source analysis: Define source files or tables for products, customers, stores, promotions, and transactions.
Phase 3 — Warehouse design: Build a star schema and define fact and dimension attributes with primary and foreign keys.
Phase 4 — ETL workflow: Extract raw retail data, clean and standardize it, compute derived metrics, and load final tables.
Phase 5 — Reporting: Create charts, KPI cards, and summary reports for faculty demonstration and viva explanation.
